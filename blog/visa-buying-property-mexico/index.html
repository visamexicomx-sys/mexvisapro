Visa for Buying Property in Mexico: What Foreigners Need to Know in 2026

Can foreigners buy property in Mexico? Yes — but depending on where and how, you may need a visa, a fideicomiso trust, or Mexican residency. Here's everything you need to know.

Published June 4, 2026 · 8 min read · MexVisa Pro

Do You Need a Visa to Buy Property in Mexico?

The short answer: no, you don't need a visa to purchase property in Mexico. Any foreigner can buy real estate in Mexico regardless of their immigration status — even as a tourist. Mexican law does not require residency to own property.

However, there are important nuances depending on where the property is located and what you plan to do with it.

The Restricted Zone: Coastal and Border Areas

Mexico's Constitution establishes a "restricted zone" (zona restringida) where foreigners cannot directly own land:

  • Within 50 km (31 miles) of the coastline
  • Within 100 km (62 miles) of international borders

This includes most of the Riviera Maya: Cancún, Playa del Carmen, Tulum, Puerto Morelos, Cozumel, and Isla Mujeres all fall within this zone.

To buy property in the restricted zone, foreigners use a fideicomiso (bank trust).

What Is a Fideicomiso?

A fideicomiso is a trust held by a Mexican bank on your behalf. You are the beneficiary with full rights to use, rent, sell, or inherit the property. The bank is the legal titleholder, but you control everything.

  • Valid for 50 years, renewable indefinitely
  • Setup cost: approximately $1,500–$2,500 USD
  • Annual fee: approximately $500–$800 USD
  • You can sell, rent, or pass the property to heirs at any time

Buying Property Outside the Restricted Zone

If the property is outside the restricted zone (for example, in Mexico City, San Miguel de Allende, or inland areas), foreigners can own property directly — no fideicomiso needed. You just need a Mexican corporation or buy in your own name.

Why Get Residency If You're Buying Property?

While you don't need residency to buy, getting Mexican residency offers significant advantages for property owners:

  • Stay longer — Tourist visas allow only 180 days. Residency lets you live in Mexico year-round.
  • Open a bank account — Required for mortgage payments, utility bills, and property management.
  • Get an RFC (tax ID) — Needed to rent your property legally and file taxes in Mexico.
  • Easier transactions — Notarios and banks deal more smoothly with residents than tourists.
  • Path to citizenship — After 5 years of residency, you can apply for Mexican citizenship.

Residency Options for Property Buyers

The most common residency paths for foreigners buying property in Mexico:

Temporary Residency (Residente Temporal)

1–4 years, renewable. Requires proof of income (~$2,500 USD/month) or savings. With MexVisa Pro's express service, you can get your residency card in 4 days.

Permanent Residency (Residente Permanente)

Indefinite stay. Available after 4 years of temporary residency, through marriage to a Mexican citizen, or by meeting higher financial requirements.

Investor Visa

For foreigners making significant investments in Mexican real estate or businesses. Requirements and thresholds vary.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Buying without a fideicomiso in the restricted zone — the sale is void under Mexican law
  • Not getting an RFC — you'll pay higher taxes on rental income without one
  • Overstaying your tourist visa — fines and immigration issues can delay your closing
  • Skipping due diligence — always verify the property title and ejido status
